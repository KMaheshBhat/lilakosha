# 2026-07-17 a week of metadata, scale, and resisting scope

The last report was written when the corpus stood at around 6k records. Today the number is 16k, (the `pippa_deduped` has 16832 records).

The raw volume growth is interesting, but what strikes me more is that the project is beginning to settle into a rhythm. There are fewer surprises in the ingestion and refinement loops. Pipelines start, run, stop, and restart. Providers come and go based on quotas and availability. The work is becoming operational.

The percentage itself is less important than the direction. At 2k records, healthy records were below 25%. At 6k they approached 50%. At 16k they have crossed 55%. The refinement pipelines are gradually consuming the backlog.

- `refine-characters`
- `refine-safety-dials`
- `refine-genre-theme`

while the local Gemma4 12B remains responsible for grammar normalization.

The bottleneck remains exactly where it was expected to be: prose conversion. The refinement itself is simple, but unlike the other stages it executes on every turn in every conversation.

One of the more significant technical changes during this period was the metadata refactor.

Originally, metadata was accumulated opportunistically by different pipeline stages. Health calculations, statistics calculations, and derived values were effectively scattered across the codebase. The introduction of a dedicated metadata lifecycle moved that logic into a centralized location and introduced a standalone `refresh-meta` step.

This was not a glamorous change, but it feels like the correct one. Metadata is now something that can be regenerated rather than something that must be preserved. The CDM itself also evolved considerably.

The earlier model was still carrying traces of the original PIPPA-centric worldview. The newer model feels much closer to a general narrative document container. The move from Session to Document was only the visible part of the change. The larger shift was the introduction of a more flexible item hierarchy and a clearer separation between source information, resolved information, health state, statistics, and annotations.

Looking back at the original CDM specification document, it now feels somewhat naive. Useful at the time, but still heavily influenced by the immediate source dataset rather than by the broader direction of the project.

Another realization this week was around scope. Several times I found myself drifting toward larger ideas: CRPO signals, rating systems, judge models, corpus-wide evaluation passes, and increasingly elaborate refinement stages.

Most of those ideas are still interesting. But, most of them are also not required for Generation 1.

The recurring lesson remains the same:

> Use the shovel. Do not build the shovel.

The project is already producing useful artifacts:

- A reusable Common Document Model
- A growing normalized corpus
- Multi-stage refinement pipelines
- Operational reporting
- A future training corpus

Those are deliverables.  That realization has helped reduce the temptation to expand every idea into a platform effort.

Open items currently include:

- Moving remote provider capacity toward grammar refinement
- Gutenberg ingestion and corpus design
- Theme clustering and relabeling

Theme clustering feels like the obvious engineering task. The problem is well understood and the expected payoff spans the entire corpus.

Gutenberg ingestion feels different. It is less about engineering and more about exploration. It will stress-test the CDM against long-form narrative works and likely expose assumptions that were inherited from conversational datasets.

One improves the current corpus.  The other expands what the corpus can become.
